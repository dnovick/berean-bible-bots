## Long Vowels

Long vowels are held for a full beat. They often include a vowel letter (mater lectionis) — aleph, he, vav, or yod — that marks the vowel length.

## Short Vowels

Short vowels are held for a half beat. Qamets Hatuf looks identical to Qamets but occurs only in a closed, unaccented syllable — context determines which is which.

## Reduced Vowels (Shewa and Hateph Vowels)

Reduced vowels are extremely short — half the duration of a short vowel. Vocal shewa (ְ) is a brief schwa sound. The three hateph vowels are used under guttural letters (א ה ח ע) instead of a simple shewa.

| # | Front (Hebrew) | Back |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | לְ | lə — Vocal Shewa (reduced) — brief schwa, like 'a' in 'alone' |
| 2 | בְּ | bə — Vocal Shewa (reduced) — brief schwa, like 'a' in 'alone' |
| 3 | אֲ | ǎ — Hateph-Patach (reduced a) — very brief 'a'; used under guttural letters |
| 4 | אֱ | ě — Hateph-Seghol (reduced e) — very brief 'e'; used under guttural letters |
| 5 | אֳ | ǒ — Hateph-Qamets (reduced o) — very brief 'o'; used under guttural letters |
